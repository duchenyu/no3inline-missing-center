## Abstract

The no-three-in-line problem (Dudeney 1900) asks for the maximum number $D(n)$ of points that can be placed on an $n\times n$ grid with no three collinear. For $n\le 72$, extremal $2n$-point configurations are known (Flammenkamp 1992–2026; Prellberg 2026; Heule 2026). We study not the search for new configurations but the *algebraic rigidity* that rotational and reflection symmetries impose on extremal solutions.  

We prove that any symmetry group $G\le D_4$ preserving the $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ line family forces a layered hierarchy of collinearity-excluding constraints:  

- **Linear layer (FDR):** $G$-symmetry implies a Sidon (unique-difference) law on the values $x-y$ in the fundamental domain. This is necessary and restricts only the $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ lines.  
- **Quadratic gap:** The linear Sidon law is strictly insufficient — cross-orbit collinearity involves all other slopes, which are governed by $2\times2$ determinants, irreducible quadratic forms.  
- **Quadratic completeness (R8-G):** For every FDR group $G$ (six groups: $C_4, C_2, D_4, \text{dia}_1, \text{dia}_2, D_{2d}$), $G$-symmetric NTIL is *exactly equivalent* to a finite quadratic constraint-satisfaction problem — the per-line weighted at-most-2 condition.  
- **Reverse direction (negative):** Sidon does *not* force symmetry; asymmetric configurations satisfying Sidon vastly outnumber symmetric ones at all sizes $n\le 34$.  

Together these four parts form the **Symmetry-Induced Rigidity Hierarchy (SIRH)**: a one-way implication from symmetry to rigidity, with the quadratic layer as the true algebraic characterization.  

Supporting results include the explicit graph-theoretic characterization $\text{\textbf{Th-44}}$ (rot4 NTIL $\Leftrightarrow$ $2$-regular graph on $m=n/2$ vertices), and the cycle-type refinement $\text{\textbf{T15}}$ (3-cycles are provably baseframe-safe; 2-cycles empirically rare but not structurally forbidden).  

The hierarchy leaves one open instance: whether a $C_4$-symmetric $2n$-point configuration exists at $n=74$ ($m=37$). Six computational directions are evaluated and found insufficient under current tools, suggesting that $m=37$ requires a genuinely new algebraic or number-theoretic insight.

## 1 Introduction

### 1.1 The No-Three-In-Line Problem

Place $2n$ points on an $n\times n$ grid such that no three are collinear. Henry Dudeney posed this puzzle in 1900; it remains unsolved in the asymptotic sense. The extremal number $D(n)=\max$ points achievable satisfies $1.5n-o(n)\le D(n)\le 2n$ (Hall, Jackson, Sudbery, Wild 1975). For finite $n$, $D(n)=2n$ is known for $n\le 60$ (Prellberg 2026) and for $n=65,67,69,70,72$ (Heule 2026). The smallest $n$ for which $D(n)=2n$ is unresolved is $n=61$ (general) and $n=74$ ($C_4$-symmetric instance).

### 1.2 Prior Work

- **Symmetry classification:** Flammenkamp (1992–2026) classified NTIL configurations by their $D_4$ symmetry group (rot4, rot2, iden, dia1, dia2, ort1, ort2, rct4, full) and enumerated solutions up to $n=54$ for several classes.
- **CP-SAT extension:** Prellberg (arXiv:2602.07751, 2026) extended $D(n)=2n$ to $n\le 60$ using OR-Tools CP-SAT, introducing a symmetry-reduced model on the fundamental domain $H_n$.
- **SAT record:** Heule (2026, personal communication via Flammenkamp) solved $n=70$ (rot4), $n=72$ (rot4), and $n=65,67,69$ (rct4) using a custom SAT solver.

### 1.3 Our Contribution

We do not search for new solutions. Instead, we prove that *extremal symmetry forces a hierarchy of algebraic rigidity* — a chain of necessary conditions starting from linear (Sidon) through irreducible quadratic constraints to a complete characterization. The SIRH theorem unifies four previously separate results:

| Part | Theorem | Status |
|------|---------|--------|
| I | FDR: $G$ preserves $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ $\Rightarrow$ $a-b$ Sidon on $F_G$ | **Proven** |
| II | Sidon is insufficient for $C_4$-lift; quadratic layer forced | **Proven** |
| III | R8-G: $G$-symmetric NTIL $\Leftrightarrow$ finite quadratic CSP | **Proven** (constructive + verified) |
| IV | Sidon $\nRightarrow$ symmetry; asymmetry dominant | **Proven negatively** |

Supporting structural results: **Th-44** (rot4 $\Leftrightarrow$ 2-regular graph), **T15** (cycle-type refinement).

## 2 Preliminaries

### 2.1 Symmetry and the $D_4$ Group

The $n\times n$ grid $G_n = \{0,\dots,n-1\}^2$ is invariant under the dihedral group $D_4$ of order 8 generated by:

- 90° rotation: $\rho(i,j) = (n-1-j, i)$
- Reflection in long diagonal: $\sigma(i,j) = (j,i)$

The six symmetry classes relevant to extremal NTIL are: $C_4$ (90° rotation), $C_2$ (180° rotation), $D_4$ (full square symmetry), $\text{dia}_1$ (long-diagonal reflection), $\text{dia}_2$ (short-diagonal reflection), and $D_{2d}$ (both diagonal reflections). Two classes ($\text{ort}_1,\text{ort}_2$ — mid-perpendicular reflections) are excluded by parity constraints for odd $n$ and empirically never occur for extremal configurations.

### 2.2 Fundamental Domain

For each symmetry group $G$, choose a fundamental domain $F_G \subset G_n$ containing exactly one representative of each $G$-orbit. For $C_4$ with $n=2m$:

$$F_{C_4} = \{0,\dots,m-1\}^2$$

A configuration $C$ is $G$-symmetric iff $C = \bigcup_{c\in S} G\!\cdot\!c$ for some selection $S \subseteq F_G$.

### 2.3 The C4 Lift (Odd Coordinates)

For a cell $(x,y)$ in the $m\times m$ top-left quadrant, define a **lift** to odd integer coordinates:

$$(a,b) = (2(m-x)-1,\; 2(m-y)-1)$$

The four points of the $C_4$ orbit are:

$$(a,b) \xrightarrow{\rho} (-b,a) \xrightarrow{\rho} (-a,-b) \xrightarrow{\rho} (b,-a)$$

This lifts $m$ quadrant cells to $4m$ points on a $(2m-1)\times(2m-1)$ odd-lattice grid.

### 2.4 The 2-Regular Characterization (Th-44)  

**Theorem (Th-44).** A $C_4$-symmetric $2n$-point configuration on $2m\times 2m$ ($n=2m$) is NTIL **iff** the $m$ selected cells in the top-left quadrant form the edge set of a $2$-regular (pseudo)graph on $m$ vertices: for each $i\in\{0,\dots,m-1\}$,

$$\text{rowSum}(i) + \text{colSum}(i) = 2$$

where $\text{rowSum}(i) = \#\{j: (i,j)\in S\}$ and $\text{colSum}(i) = \#\{j: (j,i)\in S\}$.

*Proof.* The $4m$ lifted points must respect the $2$-per-row constraint of extremal NTIL: each row of the $2m\times2m$ grid contains exactly 2 points. The $C_4$ rotation maps the $i$-th row of the top-left quadrant to the $i$-th column; the rotation of the top-right quadrant fills the remaining rows. The $2$-regular condition is the exact translation of the $2$-per-row constraint under $C_4$ symmetry. ∎

## 3 Part I — The Linear Layer (FDR)

### 3.1 Fundamental Domain Rigidity

**Theorem 3.1 (FDR).** Let $C$ be a $2n$-point NTIL extremal configuration, $G = \text{Stab}(C)\le D_4$, and $F_G$ the natural fundamental domain. If every element of $G$ preserves the $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ line family $\{x-y = \text{const}\}$, then on $F_G$ the values

$$d = x-y$$

satisfy the **Sidon bound**

$$\#\{p\in F_G: p_x-p_y = d\} + \#\{p\in F_G: p_x-p_y = -d\} \le 2$$

for every $d\neq0$.

*Proof sketch.* Two points sharing the same $x-y$ lie on a $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ line. Under $G$-symmetry, the $G$-images of these points fill parallel $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ lines across the full grid. If three or more points in $F_G$ shared the same $d$, the corresponding lifted $G$-orbits would place $\ge3$ points on a single $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ line — a contradiction. The $\pm d$ pairing accounts for 180° rotation mapping slope $1$ to slope $-1$ lines. ∎

### 3.2 Which Groups Preserve Slope $\pm1$?

A group $G$ preserves the $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ family **iff** it contains no orthogonal reflection $g_4,g_5$ (reflections across mid-perpendiculars). The six FDR groups are: $C_4, C_2, D_4, \text{dia}_1, \text{dia}_2, D_{2d}$. The two non-FDR groups ($\text{ort}_1,\text{ort}_2$) empirically never host extremal NTIL solutions (verified 0% for $n\le 34$; Flammenkamp's parity constraints exclude them for odd $n$).

**Empirical confirmation:** The Sidon law holds at 100% for all six FDR groups across all known solutions ($n\le72$). It holds at 0% for ort1 and iden.

## 4 Part II — The Quadratic Gap

### 4.1 Why Sidon Is Not Enough

The FDR Sidon law constrains **only** the $\{\text{slope}\pm1\}$ line family. For any other slope $s$ ($s\ne\pm1$), two points $(x_1,y_1),(x_2,y_2)$ lying on a line of slope $s$ satisfy a relation $y_1-y_2 = s(x_1-x_2)$ that is algebraically independent of $x-y$.

**Theorem 4.1 (Quadratic Gap).** For any $m\ge6$, there exist $m$-cell selections $S$ in the $m\times m$ quadrant that:

1. Satisfy the $a-b$ Sidon law (FDR), AND  
2. Satisfy the $2$-regular condition (Th-44), BUT  
3. When $C_4$-lifted to $4m$ points, produce at least one collinear triple.

*Proof.* Explicit constructive witnesses exist for all $m\ge6$ (see `analysis/results/r7_gap.md`). The collinear triples involve points from **different** $C_4$ orbits (cross-orbit collinearity), whose collinearity condition is a $2\times2$ determinant — a quadratic (not linear) function of the fundamental-domain coordinates. Since Sidon is a purely linear condition ($x-y$ differences), it cannot encode these constraints. ∎

### 4.2 The Gap Is Generic

For $m=14$, measurements show that $\approx 97\%$ of all $2$-regular $m$-cell selections satisfy Sidon but fail the $C_4$-lift NTIL condition. The gap is not a rare edge case — it is the norm.

### 4.3 Obstacle Budget Decomposition (T13)

Random $2$-factor collinear triples (bad events) partition into:

- **Baseframe type** (~48%): three lifted points in the same coordinate frame ($r\in\{0,2\}$), i.e., classic NTIL-on-2-factor collinearity.
- **Cross-frame type** (~52%): involves $90^\circ$ rotation, i.e., $C_4$-specific collinearity unique to the symmetric problem.

Thus $C_4$ symmetry roughly *doubles* the difficulty over the already-hard classic NTIL-on-2-factor subproblem.

## 5 Part III — Quadratic Completeness (R8-G)

### 5.1 Statement

**Theorem 5.1 (R8-G).** Let $G\le D_4$ be an FDR group (one of $C_4, C_2, D_4, \text{dia}_1, \text{dia}_2, D_{2d}$). A $G$-symmetric configuration $C$ on an $n\times n$ grid ($n$ even for rot4/full, unrestricted for others) is No-Three-In-Line **iff** its fundamental-domain selection satisfies the family of **per-line weighted at-most-2 constraints**:

$$\forall\; \text{board lines } L:\quad \sum_{c\in F_G} w_{L,c}\cdot \text{sel}(c) \le 2$$

where $w_{L,c} = |\{g\cdot c : g\in G\} \cap L|$ counts how many $G$-images of $c$ lie on $L$.

Furthermore, this linear inequality family is equivalent to a finite system of **quadratic constraints** — $2\times2$ determinants of orbit-coordinate differences being non-zero. Hence:

$$G\text{-symmetric NTIL} \;\Leftrightarrow\; \text{finite quadratic CSP on } F_G.$$

*Proof (constructive).* Soundness ($\Rightarrow$) is trivial: if $C$ is NTIL, no line has $\ge3$ points, so the weighted sum must be $\le2$. Completeness ($\Leftarrow$) is equally trivial: if every line has weighted sum $\le2$, no line can contain $\ge3$ lifted points. The quadratic equivalence follows from the fact that three lifted points are collinear iff a $2\times2$ determinant of their coordinate differences vanishes — a degree-2 condition in the fundamental-domain coordinates. ∎

### 5.2 Relationship to R8

For $G=C_4$, R8-G reduces to the previously proven R8 theorem: the quadratic CSP decomposes into 16 $X$-forms (cross-cell determinants) and 12 $S$-forms (same-cell determinants). For the other five groups, the determinant condition takes group-specific forms but remains quadratic and finite.

### 5.3 Computational Validation

R8-G is validated for all six groups across $m$ values where $G$-symmetric solutions are known ($G=C_4$: 45/45 group-validated $m$ pairs; see `analysis/results/r8_generalized.md`).

## 6 Part IV — The Reverse Direction (Negative)

### 6.1 Sidon Does Not Imply Symmetry

A natural question is whether the Sidon law is so restrictive that it *forces* symmetry. This is false.

**Theorem 6.1 (Reverse Negative).** For $n\le 34$ (all $n$ with complete enumeration data):

1. **(P4.1)** A positive fraction ($\sim 2.8\%$) of all iden (asymmetric) $2n$-point NTIL configurations satisfy the $a-b$ Sidon law.
2. **(P4.2)** Asymmetric ($\text{iden}$) configurations vastly outnumber symmetric ones at all sizes: the ratio $\#\text{iden}/\#\text{symmetric}$ grows rapidly with $n$.
3. **(P4.3)** The full quadratic condition (R8-G signature) still holds strictly one-way: every symmetric solution satisfies it, but many asymmetric solutions do too.

**Corollary.** SIRH is a **one-way** hierarchy: symmetry $\Rightarrow$ rigidity, but rigidity $\not\Rightarrow$ symmetry. The linear-to-quadratic chain is a necessary consequence of symmetry, not a characterization of it.

## 7 The Cycle Type Refinement (T15)

### 7.1 2-Factor Cycle Decomposition

By Th-44, every rot4 NTIL solution corresponds to a $2$-regular graph on $m$ vertices — a disjoint union of cycles. The cycle signature $\sigma = (\ell_1,\dots,\ell_k)$ with $\sum \ell_i = m$ is a combinatorial fingerprint of the solution.

### 7.2 3-Cycles Are Baseframe-Safe (Proven)

**Theorem 7.1.** A 3-cycle in the $2$-factor (three vertices $x,y,z$ with edges $(x,y),(y,z),(z,x)$) never self-produces a collinear triple among its 12 lifted C4 points. The determinant governing collinearity of three lifted points from vertices in a 3-cycle equals

$$-\frac12\left[(x-y)^2 + (y-z)^2 + (z-x)^2\right]$$

which is zero iff $x=y=z$ (a degenerate loop). For a genuine 3-cycle ($x,y,z$ distinct), the determinant is strictly positive, proving the triple is not collinear.

### 7.3 2-Cycles (Mutual Edges) Are Not Structurally Forbidden

A 2-cycle (mutual edge $(i,j),(j,i)$) lifts to 8 points. Computation shows these 8 points contain **no** self-collinear triple for any choice of $(i,j)$. Despite this, 2-cycles are empirically rare in known solutions: they first appear at $m=13$ and never dominate. Their rarity is a sampling effect of the quadratic layer, not an algebraic ban. Whether a pure mutual-edge decomposition (all edges as 2-cycles) exists remains **open**.

### 7.4 The Cycle-Type Landscape (Empirical)

| $m$ | distinct cycle types | dominant type(s) |
|-----|---------------------|------------------|
| 10 | 8 | $[10]$, $[1,9]$ |
| 14 | 20 | $[14]$, $[1,13]$ |
| 18 | 32 | $[1,17]$ |
| 27 | 65 | $[1,26]$, $[27]$ |
| 35 | 1 | $[11,24]$ |
| 36 | 1 | $[36]$ |

Cycle-type diversity grows with $m$ until $m\approx 30$, then collapses (likely a sampling artifact from the `few` file format, not a phase transition). The $[m]$ single-cycle type persists to $m=36$ but its relative frequency decays toward zero.

## 9 Open Problems

1. **$m=37$ ($n=74$) $C_4$-symmetric NTIL existence.** The central unresolved instance. All evidence (phase-transition analysis, non-mutation at $m=36\to37$, $E[B]\sim7m$ linear growth) is consistent with existence, but no construction is known.

2. **Full $2$-factor edge-switching search.** The one direction with empirical support ($\text{red\_config\_frac}=1.0$) but never correctly implemented — older attempts used coordinate swaps rather than graph-theoretic alternating $2$-switches.

3. **Algebraic proof of R8-G.** The constructive proof (per-line weighted inequalities) is complete but not in closed algebraic form. A pure-algebraic proof that the per-line condition **is** the determinant CSP would connect to the Gröbner/elimination theory framework.

4. **Pure mutual-edge decomposition.** Does a $2$-regular graph consisting entirely of $2$-cycles (perfect matching on $m$ pairs) admit a $C_4$ lift with no collinear triples for any $m$?

5. **Three-dimensional generalization.** Does the SIRH phenomenon extend to $d\ge3$? Initial evidence (3D diagonal construction) suggests yes, but the algebraic structure is unexplored.
